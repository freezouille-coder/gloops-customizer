Game Design Document — v0.2 Draft
Gloops World
Browser-based monster collection & idle management game
Platform Web (browser) Audience Casual, ages 12–35 Genre Idle / Collection / Breeding Status Pre-production
01Vision & concept

Gloops World is a casual browser game where players collect, hatch and breed small monsters called Gloops — each carrying heritable traits and belonging to one of two factions: the Aveggies (nature lovers, gardeners, whole-food advocates) and the Sugar Demons (junk food addicts, industrial chemists, candy manufacturers).

Once assembled, Gloops are assigned to buildings that generate currency continuously — provided the player fields the right team. The core challenge: finding which Gloop goes where to maximize production, complete the collection, and win faction wars.

One-sentence pitch: Tamagotchi meets idle factory — breed genetically unique monsters, put them to work in a village, and come back to watch them produce.

Gameplay pillars

🥚
Collection
Discover, unlock and collect rare Gloops — from eggs, breeding, merging, or special events.
🧬
Breeding
Breed Gloops to produce offspring with new trait combos via dominant/recessive inheritance. Each Gloop can only have 3 children total.
🏗️
Management
Assign the right Gloops to the right buildings to maximize coin production. Wrong match = underperforming building.
🔀
Merging
Combine 3 Gloops of the same rarity into one of the next tier. Shared traits are guaranteed in the result.
⚔️
Faction wars
Timed collective events — players pick a side and contribute to a shared production goal.
02Factions

Every Gloop belongs to one of two factions. Faction determines which buildings they can power efficiently and which side they fight for during events.

🌿 Aveggies
  • Gardeners, herbalists, farmers
  • Produce vegetables, juices, organic goods
  • Efficient in nature-based buildings
  • Ineffective in sugar factories
  • Visual: green tones, round eyes, soft shapes
🍬 Sugar Demons
  • Chemists, confectioners, industrial cooks
  • Produce sodas, candy, chips
  • Efficient in sugar-based factories
  • Ineffective in gardens and farms
  • Visual: yellow/orange tones, slit eyes, spikes
Design note: a Gloop assigned to an incompatible building doesn't produce zero — it produces less. The game stays readable while rewarding optimization.
03Gloops — traits & tools

Each Gloop is defined by its faction, rarity tier, and tools. Tools are functional tags that determine which buildings a Gloop can unlock or power at full efficiency.

Legendary and epic Gloops correspond to characters from the animated series. They use fixed assets — unlike commons which are procedurally generated.

Genetics (breeding)

Each Gloop has two tool slots: one dominant (passed to ~75% of offspring) and one recessive (passed to ~25%). A ~5% mutation chance can introduce a trait absent from both parents.

Breeding constraint: each Gloop can only produce 3 children total across all pairings. This creates real strategic weight on rare Gloops — they are non-renewable.
04Merging — end-game progression

When a player has exhausted breeding potential or filled all building slots, merging provides a long-term progression layer. Combine 3 Gloops of the same rarity to produce 1 Gloop of the next tier.

Full chain from Common to Legendary requires 81 commons (3⁴). Each step consumes 3 Gloops of one tier to produce 1 of the next. This keeps dedicated players engaged for months without forcing a content reset.

Trait inheritance on merge

If all 3 Gloops being merged share a common tool tag, that tag is guaranteed in the resulting Gloop. All other traits follow the standard dominant/recessive rules.

Strategic implication: players consciously select which 3 Gloops to merge based on the trait they want to lock in. A common Gloop with a desirable rare trait becomes a resource in itself — and creates implicit trade value if a player exchange system is added later.
Design tension: merging consumes Gloops permanently. A rare Gloop with good traits that gets merged is gone. This gives weight to every merge decision and prevents inventory from becoming meaningless.
05Eggs & hatching

Players start with 3 eggs. Each egg contains a Gloop whose traits are determined at acquisition but revealed only at hatching.

06Buildings & production

All buildings are placed on the island — a fixed plot with a limited number of slots. The village grows organically as the player purchases new buildings.

Buildings are the core of the economic loop. Each has assignment slots, trait requirements, and a production cap that forces the player to return and collect.

Key retention mechanic: each building has a maximum storage. Once full, it stops producing. Players must return to collect — but no punishment for coming back late (they lose nothing, they just stagnate).

07Economy & monetization

Standard currency (Gloops Coins — GC)

Earned through building production. Spent on:

Buy eggs Breed Gloops Buy buildings Upgrade buildings

Premium currency (Gloops Gems — GG)

Purchased with real money. Not exchangeable for GC. Spent on:

Cosmetics (skins, accessories) Instant hatching Boosted rarity eggs Extra slots
Core principle: Gems cannot buy raw power — they buy speed or aesthetics. A non-paying player can unlock everything, just more slowly.

Leaderboard

Global ranking by total GC earned lifetime. The score cannot be purchased — it reflects playtime and optimization only.

Complete the Gloopédex — track down all rare and legendary Gloops, including characters from the animated series. This requires strategic breeding over weeks and merging chains over months. Legendary Gloops have a dedicated showcase slot in the UI.

10Design decisions

Settled questions — these inform the current design.

  • ✓OnboardingThe first breeding takes 30–60 seconds. The first building is purchased immediately after, and the player can assign their first Gloop right away — even suboptimally. Speed of first feedback loop is the priority.
  • ✓BreedingThe 3-children limit applies to all Gloops including starters. No exceptions.
  • ✓CollectionLegendary Gloops (series characters) are obtainable only through rare breeding chains or merging. No event-exclusive legendaries.
  • ✓BuildingsBuilding upgrades are Phase 2. MVP ships with fixed building levels.
  • ✓MonetizationNo battle pass. Players purchase Gloops Gems directly and spend them in-game (instant hatching, cosmetics, boosted eggs).
  • ✓DistributionStandalone URL shared via social media. No integration with an existing account system at launch.
11Open design questions

Two unresolved questions with significant impact on the core design — to be discussed.

How are Gloops differentiated beyond faction?

The existing generated assets need a trait system that maps to gameplay. Two main directions:

Option A — Tool tags (current direction)
Each Gloop carries functional tags (Gardening, Chemistry, etc.) that determine building compatibility. Simple, visual, immediately readable for casual players. The downside: less numerical depth for optimizers.
Option B — Stats (Strength, Endurance…)
Each Gloop has numerical attributes that affect production rate or efficiency. More granular optimization, familiar to RPG players. Risk: harder to read at a glance, higher design complexity.
Option C — Hybrid
Tool tags determine building compatibility (gating), stats determine efficiency within a compatible slot. Best of both — but also most complex to design and communicate to a casual audience.
Current lean: tool tags alone (Option A). Simpler to build, simpler to read, better fit for a casual web audience. Stats can be layered in Phase 2 if depth is needed.

How does faction work for the player?

A player will inevitably accumulate Gloops from both factions through breeding and merging. Several ways to handle this:

Option A — Two separate trees
Aveggies Gloops and Sugar Demons Gloops evolve in parallel, independently. Clean narrative, but the player may feel stuck with useless Gloops from the "wrong" side.
Option B — Conversion mechanic
Spend GC (or GG) to convert an Aveggie to Sugar Demon and vice versa. Solves the useless-Gloop problem, adds a spending sink, but risks diluting faction identity.
Option C — Neutral buildings
Expand the neutral building category so off-faction Gloops always have somewhere to go. No conversion needed, lower friction — but weakens the strategic weight of faction.
Option D — Cross-faction buildings
High-tier buildings require one Aveggie + one Sugar Demon in the same team. Forces players to maintain both factions, turns the "problem" into a design feature.
Option E — Faction fluidity via events
During faction war events, players temporarily align with one side. Between events, all Gloops can work all buildings at reduced efficiency. Faction is a choice, not a permanent identity.
Note: Option D (cross-faction buildings) pairs well with the tool tag system and creates the most interesting assignment puzzles — worth exploring first.
